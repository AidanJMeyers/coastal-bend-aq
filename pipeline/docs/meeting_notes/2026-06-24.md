## 2. The instrumentation problem (dominant discussion)

**The single most important technical concern raised:** monitors and
their reference methods get replaced over a 10-year study period, and
cross-year machine-learning models trained on mixed instrumentation
can spuriously attribute an instrument-baseline shift to a real
pollution trend. Key excerpts:

- Aidan: "Now try to picture your timeline. Even if you had only eight
  sites, try to picture your timeline. Now, five years in, the sensor
  was changed or the reference method was changed ... What was the
  reason it got replaced? Do those numbers, are they still considered
  validated? Do those get included in the same machine-learning
  training set?"
- Aidan flagged the specific example of PM10 at CC Holly: **method 141
  (1-in-6 day FRM filter, 2015-2018) → 5-year gap (2019-2023) →
  method 639 (continuous BAM, 2024-2025).** "These do change and
  ... it could tweak the prediction. You know what I'm saying? So the
  goal, even if we have eight sites, is to figure out of the eight,
  what can we compare between, what actually feeds in."
- Aidan on why this matters for method rigor: "That was another thing
  that happened at the end of the time series — is we had all these
  nice little correlations and everything else and no one thought
  about the method, no one thought about the instrumentation, no one
  thought about commenting on it. And all it ended up being was a
  table of being reported with no commenting on it. Like I literally
  wrote FRM and FEM having no clue what they meant, just so that we
  had them down."
- Jasmine confirmed she knows about instrumentation regulations (e.g.,
  anemometer siting: ≥100 ft from a building) but not the specific TCEQ
  method-code semantics.

**Resolution:** every pollutant lead audits method-code changes for

## 4. Data-source strategy

- **TCEQ-only forever.** EPA AQS API integration retired at the
  south-texas-aq v0.4.0 milestone (2026-05-28). Aidan's rationale
  restated in the meeting: TCEQ reports hourly data directly; EPA's
  Data Mart re-serves 8hr / 24hr / 3-day aggregates that are the
  wrong shape for meteorologically-anchored machine-learning models.
- **Data cadence:** hourly wherever possible. From Aidan: "It's the
  strongest for imputation ... it definitely is going to give us more
  variables to go through." Jasmine confirmed: "The smaller the
  resolution, the better. It's always like that."
- **Weather data:** unchanged from upstream south-texas-aq pipeline
  (OpenWeather One Call + Solcast). Jasmine's meteorology framing:
  Coastal Bend meteorology is **dominated by the persistent sea breeze,
  winter northerlies, and the ENSO cycle** — "the meteorological
  factors, I'm not too concerned about considering how consistent it
  is here."
- **Precipitation:** rare in the Coastal Bend. Just came out of a
  5-6 year drought cycle; recently entered La Niña wet phase. Not a
  routine daily driver.
