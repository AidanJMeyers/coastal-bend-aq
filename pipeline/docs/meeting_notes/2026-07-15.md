## 1. Jasmine — pollution rose idea

**What she proposed.** Wind rose diagrams (compass-rose plots showing wind
frequency + direction over time) reinterpreted as **pollution roses** —
directional plots showing where each pollutant is heading, colored by AQI
severity level. This differs from a standard wind rose in that it foregrounds
the pollutant's implied trajectory, not just the meteorology.

**Implementation path (Manasa's find later in the meeting):** the R
[`openair`](https://davidcarslaw.github.io/openair/) package has a built-in
pollution-rose function. No custom-built stack needed for a prototype.

**Coastal-Bend advantage — sea-breeze consistency.** Jasmine confirmed that
the wind pattern is remarkably consistent across the entire 11-county Coastal
Bend region (verified this at the NWS office recently; forecasters do not need
to re-analyze wind direction for different Coastal-Bend sites — it is
essentially uniform). This means a **single pollution rose per pollutant per
season for the whole region is defensible**. Only when we push into San
Antonio or deep Rio Grande south does the wind direction diverge enough to
require separate roses.

**Seasonality expectation (strong prior).**

- Winter — northern cold fronts inject cold dry air; different rose signature.
- Summer — Gulf sea breeze dominates; different rose signature.
- Diurnal patterns (day vs night) exist but likely out of scope for a first
  paper.

**Scientific gap Jasmine flagged.** Coastal-breeze modification of
pollution-weather relationships is under-quantified in the literature. Aidan
added: *"There's a lot of hypothesis out there as far as what coastal breeze
may influence or pose. There's not a lot of scientific evidence that really
explains how it modifies a traditional relationship that you might expect."*

**Phase-shift theory (Aidan).** Referenced a Chinese COVID-19 paper (from
Dr. Miller's introduction earlier in the semester) claiming the pandemic didn't
just immediately affect pollutant patterns but *phase-shifted the seasonal
wave months forward*. Multi-year pollution roses would let us test whether
that phase-shift is visible in the Coastal Bend.

**Data availability check.** Best coverage for pollution-rose analysis:
Ozone + SO₂ (CC West + CC Tuloso 2015–2025 for Ozone; SO₂ at 3 active sites +
CC Holly through mid-2017). PM2.5 more limited. VOCs 2025-only in current
pull — VOC retro-pull decision becomes more urgent.

## 2. Manasa — refinery-centered framing

After Jasmine left the call, Manasa (having briefly researched pollution
roses during the meeting itself) suggested that **centering the pollution-rose
analysis at the refinery corridor** would extract the most scientific signal:

> *"Which direction is the ozone passing through the oil refineries towards the
> bayside? Or which direction is ozone / PM2.5 / any air pollutant passing
> through? We could understand that factor in the region."*

She also flagged the temporal dimension: with pollution rose + wind speed +
timestamp, we can estimate *when* a plume reaches a downwind location.

## 3. Aidan — the crystallized proposal

Aidan built directly on Manasa's suggestion in real-time. The core idea:

> **Not just county-level or regional health data — residentially-zoned health
> outcomes correlated with wind direction from Refinery Row at that time,
> across time points. Then run a machine-learning predictive model where the
> wind-direction-from-Refinery-Row × pollutant flow at time T predicts health
> exacerbations at the downwind zone at time T + Δ.**

**Why this is different from every other air-pollution epi study:**

1. **Directional pollution flow** as the exposure axis instead of point
   concentrations.
2. **Refinery Row as a real, identifiable point source** — this converts a
   diffuse regional environmental study into a **point-source attribution
   study**, which is much stronger for policy.
3. **Temporal resolution of the industrial-source effect** at day / week /
   month scale, not just seasonal averages.
4. **Residential zone × time × wind direction × pollutant × health outcome** —
   5-dimensional analysis, not just 3.
5. **Health outcome as the ML target**, not the pollutant concentration.

**Impact reasoning (Aidan verbatim):** *"This is a paper that would have
gigantic implications on a government level, policy level. And it's something
that directly illustrates the effect of an industrial presence, more so than
what we've been doing with just basic correlations or general regional
hotspots that aren't time-located."*

**Result-robust framing.** A *null* finding is also publishable — it would
speak to whether the **coastal breeze is protective** for industrial-corridor
communities, with zoning / industrial-siting policy implications either way.
